% ABSTRACT
% ===================================================================
\begin{abstract}
The Spielman--Teng conjecture predicted that for an $n \times n$ random matrix $M$
with iid subgaussian entries, $\Prob(\sigma_n(M) \le \varepsilon n^{-1/2}) \le C\varepsilon + e^{-cn}$.
The best prior results left a gap: the constant $C$ couldn't be pushed to~$1$.
In 2024, Sah, Sahasrabudhe, and Sawhney proved the conjecture up to a $1 + o(1)$ factor,
showing $\Prob(\sigma_n(M) \le \varepsilon n^{-1/2}) \le (1+o(1))\varepsilon + e^{-\Omega(n)}$.
These notes walk through their proof architecture and verify the bound computationally.
\end{abstract}

% 1. INTRODUCTION

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

I first encountered the Spielman--Teng conjecture in Zixiang's office hours, scribbled
on the corner of a whiteboard that hadn't been fully erased. He was talking about smoothed
analysis and why the simplex method works so well in practice. The question on the board
was about the smallest singular value of a random matrix. How small can it be?

The answer, it turns out, has a long history. For an $n \times n$ matrix $A$, the condition
number
\[
  \kappa(A) = \frac{\sigmamax(A)}{\sigmamin(A)}
\]
governs everything from the stability of linear solvers to convergence rates of
iterative methods. Worst-case condition numbers can be absurd. The $50 \times 50$
Hilbert matrix has $\kappa(H_{50}) > 10^{17}$. But in practice, matrices that arise
from real data or random perturbations of structured inputs behave far better.

Spielman and Teng~\cite{spielman2004smoothed} formalized this intuition with smoothed
analysis, which asks: given an adversarially chosen matrix $M$ and a small random
perturbation $\sigma G$, how large is $\E[\kappa(M + \sigma G)]$? They conjectured
the answer is $O(n/\sigma)$, regardless of the base matrix $M$. The conjecture
sat open for nearly two decades. Partial progress came from Sankar, Spielman, and
Teng~\cite{sankar2006smoothed}, who proved $O(n^{3/2}/\sigma)$, and from Tao and
Vu~\cite{tao2008condition}, who got $O(n/\sigma)$ with logarithmic losses.

But the question that mattered more, at least from a random matrix theory perspective,
was sharper. Forget smoothed analysis for a moment. Take $M$ to be a random matrix with
iid entries. What's the probability that its least singular value $\sigma_n(M)$ is
tiny?

Edelman~\cite{edelman1988eigenvalues} answered this exactly for Gaussian matrices in 1988.
The rescaled quantity $n \cdot \sigma_n(M)$ converges in distribution to a random variable
with density
\begin{equation}
  \label{eq:edelman-density}
  f(x) = (x + 1) e^{-x^2/2 - x}, \quad x \ge 0.
\end{equation}
This gives $\Prob(\sigma_n(G) \le \varepsilon n^{-1/2}) \approx \varepsilon$ for
small $\varepsilon$, where $G$ is Gaussian.

For non-Gaussian entries, Rudelson and Vershynin~\cite{rudelson2008littlewood,rudelson2009smallest}
proved the bound
\begin{equation}
  \label{eq:rv-bound}
  \Prob\!\left(\sigma_n(M) \le \frac{\varepsilon}{\sqrt{n}}\right) \le C\varepsilon + e^{-cn},
\end{equation}
where $C > 1$ is an absolute constant. Tao and Vu~\cite{tao2010random} improved
this to $C = 1$ for $\varepsilon \ge n^{-c}$, but the full range remained out of reach.
Tikhomirov~\cite{tikhomirov2020singularity} made a breakthrough of a different kind,
proving that the singularity probability of a random Rademacher ($\pm 1$) matrix is
exactly $2^{-n + o(n)}$.

The Spielman--Teng conjecture, in its sharpest form, asked for $C = 1$. Sah, Sahasrabudhe,
and Sawhney~\cite{sah2024spielman} proved it, up to a $1 + o(1)$ factor. That's what these
notes are about.

The paper is organized as follows. Section~\ref{sec:prelim} collects definitions and
tools. Section~\ref{sec:main-result} states the main theorems. Section~\ref{sec:proof-arch}
walks through the four-step proof, which is the heart of these notes. Section~\ref{sec:experiments}
describes Monte Carlo experiments that verify the bound empirically.
Section~\ref{sec:discussion} discusses what's left.

% 2. PRELIMINARIES
% ===================================================================
\section{Preliminaries}
\label{sec:prelim}

\subsection{Subgaussian Random Variables}

\begin{definition}
A centered random variable $\xi$ is \emph{subgaussian} with parameter $K$ if
$\E[e^{t\xi}] \le e^{K^2 t^2 / 2}$ for all $t \in \R$. The subgaussian norm is
$\|\xi\|_{\psi_2} = \inf\{K > 0 : \E[e^{\xi^2/K^2}] \le 2\}$.
\end{definition}

Standard Gaussians are subgaussian. So are Rademacher random variables ($\pm 1$
with equal probability), bounded random variables, and more generally any random
variable whose tails decay at least as fast as a Gaussian's. A detailed treatment
is in Vershynin~\cite{vershynin2018high}, Chapters 2--3.

\subsection{Singular Values}

For an $n \times n$ matrix $M$, the singular values $\sigma_1(M) \ge \sigma_2(M) \ge
\cdots \ge \sigma_n(M) \ge 0$ are the eigenvalues of $(M^* M)^{1/2}$. The least
singular value $\sigma_n(M)$ equals the distance from $M$ to the set of
singular matrices:
\[
  \sigma_n(M) = \min_{\|x\| = 1} \|Mx\| = \inf\{t \ge 0 : M - tI \text{ is singular for some rotation}\}.
\]
When $M$ has iid entries of mean~0 and variance~$1/n$, the global distribution of
singular values follows the Mar\v{c}enko--Pastur law~\cite{marchenko1967distribution}.
The edge behavior---the largest and smallest singular values---is more delicate.

\subsection{Covering Numbers and $\varepsilon$-Nets}

A standard tool in the analysis of random matrices is the $\varepsilon$-net argument.
An $\varepsilon$-net of the unit sphere $S^{n-1}$ is a finite set $\mathcal{N}$ such
that every point in $S^{n-1}$ lies within distance $\varepsilon$ of some point in
$\mathcal{N}$. The covering number $\mathcal{N}(S^{n-1}, \varepsilon)$ satisfies
\[
  \left(\frac{1}{\varepsilon}\right)^n \le \mathcal{N}(S^{n-1}, \varepsilon)
  \le \left(\frac{2}{\varepsilon} + 1\right)^n.
\]
This lets you discretize the unit sphere and reduce continuous optimization
problems (like bounding $\sigma_n(M)$) to finite union bounds. See
Vershynin~\cite{vershynin2018high}, Chapter~4.

% 3. THE MAIN RESULT
% ===================================================================
\section{The Main Result}
\label{sec:main-result}

Let $M$ be an $n \times n$ matrix with iid entries drawn from a subgaussian distribution
$\xi$ with mean~$0$ and variance~$1$. The main result of Sah, Sahasrabudhe, and
Sawhney~\cite{sah2024spielman} is:

\begin{theorem}[Sah--Sahasrabudhe--Sawhney, Theorem 1.1]
\label{thm:sss-main}
For all $\varepsilon \ge 0$,
\begin{equation}
  \label{eq:sss-main}
  \Prob\!\left(\sigma_n(M) \le \frac{\varepsilon}{\sqrt{n}}\right)
  \le (1 + o(1))\varepsilon + e^{-\Omega_\xi(n)},
\end{equation}
where $o(1)$ decays as $C_\xi (\log n)^{-1/16}$.
\end{theorem}

The implied constant in $\Omega_\xi(n)$ and the constant $C_\xi$ depend on the
subgaussian norm of the entry distribution $\xi$, but not on $\varepsilon$ or on
the specific distribution beyond its first two moments and subgaussian parameter.

The result also establishes universality:

\begin{theorem}[Sah--Sahasrabudhe--Sawhney, Theorem 1.2]
\label{thm:sss-universality}
Let $G$ be an $n \times n$ matrix with iid $N(0,1)$ entries. Then
\begin{equation}
  \label{eq:sss-universality}
  \Prob\!\left(\sigma_n(M) \le \frac{\varepsilon}{\sqrt{n}}\right)
  = (1 + o(1))\,\Prob\!\left(\sigma_n(G) \le \frac{\varepsilon}{\sqrt{n}}\right)
  + e^{-\Omega_\xi(n)}.
\end{equation}
\end{theorem}

Since the Gaussian case is known exactly via Edelman's density, this
gives:

\begin{corollary}[Corollary 1.3]
\label{cor:sss}
For $0 \le \varepsilon \ll 1$,
\begin{equation}
  \label{eq:sss-corollary}
  \Prob\!\left(\sigma_n(M) \le \frac{\varepsilon}{\sqrt{n}}\right)
  = (1 + o(1))\varepsilon + e^{-\Omega_\xi(n)}.
\end{equation}
\end{corollary}

\begin{remark}
The $o(1)$ term decaying as $(\log n)^{-1/16}$ is probably not optimal.
Sah, Sahasrabudhe, and Sawhney note that their argument loses a logarithmic factor
at the truncation step (Section~\ref{sec:truncation}), and improving the exponent $-1/16$
would require tighter control on the regularity events. Whether the $o(1)$ can be
removed entirely---giving $C = 1$ on the nose---remains open.
\end{remark}

% 4. PROOF ARCHITECTURE
% ===================================================================
\section{Proof Architecture}
\label{sec:proof-arch}

The proof of Theorem~\ref{thm:sss-main} proceeds in four steps. I'll walk
through each one, focusing on the ideas rather than the technical details.
The full argument spans Sections 3--9 of~\cite{sah2024spielman}.

\subsection{Step 1: Geometric Reduction}
\label{sec:geometric}

Write $M = \begin{pmatrix} M^* \\ X^T \end{pmatrix}$, where $M^*$ is the
$(n-1) \times n$ matrix consisting of the first $n-1$ rows and $X \in \R^n$
is the last row. The key observation is a rank-1 update formula that connects
$\sigma_n(M)$ to the geometry of $X$ relative to $M^*$.

\begin{lemma}[Rank-1 update, Lemma 3.2 of~\cite{sah2024spielman}]
\label{lem:rank1}
Let $v \in \ker(M^*)$ be a unit vector (assuming $M^*$ has rank $n-1$, which
happens with high probability). Let $u_1, \ldots, u_{n-1}$ be right singular
vectors of $M^*$ with corresponding singular values $\sigma_1(M^*) \ge \cdots
\ge \sigma_{n-1}(M^*)$. Then
\[
  \sigma_n(M)^2 = \frac{|\langle v, X \rangle|^2}{1 + \sum_{i=1}^{n-1}
  \frac{\langle u_i, X \rangle^2}{\sigma_i(M^*)^2}}.
\]
\end{lemma}

The denominator is essentially $\tilde{\chi}(X)^2$, a weighted sum that measures
how much $X$ correlates with the singular directions of $M^*$, weighted inversely
by the corresponding singular values. So $\sigma_n(M) \le \varepsilon n^{-1/2}$
translates to
\[
  |\langle v, X \rangle| \le (1 + \varepsilon^{1/4}) \varepsilon n^{-1/2} \tilde{\chi}(X).
\]
This is a geometric statement: the projection of the last row onto the kernel of $M^*$
is small relative to a chi-type statistic.

The power of this reduction is that it separates the randomness of $X$ from the
randomness of $M^*$. Conditional on $M^*$, the vector $v$ and the singular structure
are fixed, and we just need to control $|\langle v, X \rangle|$ relative to
$\tilde{\chi}(X)$. This is an anti-concentration problem.

\subsection{Step 2: Truncation}
\label{sec:truncation}

The chi-type statistic $\tilde{\chi}(X)$ involves all $n-1$ singular directions of $M^*$.
The small singular values create large terms in the sum, and these are hard to control.
The idea is to truncate: replace $\tilde{\chi}$ with a truncated version $\chi$ that
only sums over the $\ell = \sqrt{\log n}$ smallest singular directions.

\begin{definition}
Define the truncated chi statistic
\[
  \chi(X)^2 = 1 + \sum_{i=n-\ell}^{n-1} \frac{\langle u_i, X \rangle^2}{\sigma_i(M^*)^2},
\]
where $\ell = \lfloor \sqrt{\log n} \rfloor$.
\end{definition}

The truncation introduces an error, and controlling this error requires a \emph{regularity
event} $R = R_1 \cap R_2 \cap R_3 \cap R_4$ that constrains the singular value behavior
of $M^*$. The four sub-events are:
\begin{itemize}
  \item $R_1$: $\sigma_{n-1}(M^*) \ge (\log n)^{-3} n^{-1/2}$. The second-to-last
    singular value isn't too small.
  \item $R_2$: For all $k$, $|\langle v_{n-k}, X \rangle| \le \max\{k^{1/8}, \log\log n\}$
    and $\sigma_{n-k}(M^*) \ge k^{3/4} n^{-1/2}$. The projections and singular values
    are well-behaved at all scales.
  \item $R_3, R_4$: Technical control on the $(\log\log n)^2$ scale, ensuring that
    the truncated chi doesn't deviate too much from the full chi.
\end{itemize}

Sah, Sahasrabudhe, and Sawhney show (Sections 4--7 of~\cite{sah2024spielman}) that
\begin{equation}
  \label{eq:truncation}
  \Prob\!\left(\sigma_n(M) \le \frac{\varepsilon}{\sqrt{n}} \text{ and } R^c\right)
  = O(\delta_n \varepsilon),
\end{equation}
where $\delta_n = (\log n)^{-c}$ for some constant $c > 0$. In other words, the
``bad'' event where regularity fails and the singular value is small contributes
only $o(\varepsilon)$ to the probability.

The proofs of these regularity estimates are the longest part of the paper. They
require careful bounds on the singular value gaps of $(n-1) \times n$ random
matrices. The key input is a bound on $\sigma_{n-k}(M^*)$ that gives
polynomial-in-$k$ growth away from the edge. Getting this right is where the
$(\log n)^{-1/16}$ loss comes from. The exponent $-1/16$ arises from
balancing several error terms against each other: truncation level $\ell$,
regularity failure probability, and the approximation quality in the Lindeberg
step.

\subsection{Step 3: Gaussian Replacement}
\label{sec:gaussian-replacement}

This is the conceptual core. On the regularity event $R$, we've reduced to controlling
\[
  \Prob_X\!\left(|\langle v, X \rangle| \le (1 + \delta_n)\varepsilon n^{-1/2} \chi(X)\right).
\]
The truncated chi $\chi(X)$ depends on $X$ through only $\ell = \sqrt{\log n}$
projections, and the regularity event ensures these projections are well-controlled.
The goal is to replace $X$ with a Gaussian vector $Z$ and show the probabilities
are close.

The tool is Lindeberg exchange. Replace the entries of $X$ one at a time with
Gaussian entries. At each step, the change in probability is controlled by a
Taylor expansion. The truncation to $\ell$ directions is what makes this work:
the function we're bounding the probability of depends on $X$ through $\langle v, X \rangle$
and $\ell$ other projections, and these projections change by at most $O(1)$ at
each replacement step.

Sah, Sahasrabudhe, and Sawhney use ``negative correlation inequalities'' from
Campos, Jenssen, Michelen, and Sahasrabudhe~\cite{campos2023negative}. These
inequalities control the anti-concentration of linear forms in random variables
that may be weakly correlated. The result (Section 8 of~\cite{sah2024spielman}) is:
\begin{equation}
  \label{eq:lindeberg}
  \Prob_X\!\left(|\langle v, X \rangle| \le (1 + \delta_n)\varepsilon n^{-1/2} \chi(X)\right)
  \le \Prob_Z\!\left(|\langle v, Z \rangle| \le (1 + 2\delta_n)\varepsilon n^{-1/2} \chi(Z)\right)
  + o(\varepsilon).
\end{equation}
The Gaussian case on the right is now tractable: $\langle v, Z \rangle$ is a standard
Gaussian (since $v$ is a unit vector and $Z$ has iid $N(0,1)$ entries), and $\chi(Z)$
is a chi-type random variable that's independent of $\langle v, Z \rangle$ when
conditioned on $M^*$.

A subtlety. The Lindeberg exchange doesn't work directly on the original (untruncated)
chi statistic $\tilde{\chi}$, because the full sum involves all $n-1$ singular
directions and the error from each replacement step doesn't decay fast enough. The
truncation to $\ell = \sqrt{\log n}$ directions is precisely what makes the total
Lindeberg error $o(\varepsilon)$.

\subsection{Step 4: Rescaling and Universality}
\label{sec:rescaling}

After Gaussian replacement, we have a bound in terms of the Gaussian singular value
probability. The final step passes from the truncated-chi formulation back to a
singular value statement.

Set $\varepsilon_0 = n^{-c'}$ for a small constant $c'$. On the Gaussian side,
Tao--Vu universality for row replacement gives
\[
  \Prob\!\left(\sigma_n(\tilde{M}) \le \frac{\varepsilon_0}{\sqrt{n}}\right) + o(\varepsilon),
\]
where $\tilde{M}$ is the matrix $M$ with its last row replaced by a Gaussian row.
Since $\sigma_n(G)$ has the Edelman distribution (which is universal for the
$n^{-1/2}$-scale behavior), this yields
\[
  \Prob\!\left(\sigma_n(M) \le \frac{\varepsilon}{\sqrt{n}}\right)
  \le (1 + o(1))\,\Prob\!\left(\sigma_n(G) \le \frac{\varepsilon}{\sqrt{n}}\right)
  + e^{-\Omega(n)}.
\]
Plugging in $\Prob(\sigma_n(G) \le \varepsilon n^{-1/2}) \le \varepsilon$
(from Edelman) gives the main bound.

The rescaling step also yields Theorem~\ref{thm:sss-universality} directly: the
subgaussian probability is $(1 + o(1))$ times the Gaussian probability, so the
least singular value distribution is universal up to the $o(1)$ factor.

\subsection{Summary of the Architecture}

\begin{center}
\fbox{\parbox{0.9\textwidth}{
\textbf{Proof outline:}
\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Geometric reduction.} Use rank-1 update to reduce $\sigma_n(M)
    \le \varepsilon n^{-1/2}$ to an anti-concentration statement about
    $|\langle v, X \rangle|$ relative to $\tilde{\chi}(X)$.
  \item \textbf{Truncation.} Replace $\tilde{\chi}$ with truncated $\chi$
    (summing over $\sqrt{\log n}$ smallest directions). On a high-probability
    regularity event $R$, the error is $o(\varepsilon)$.
  \item \textbf{Gaussian replacement.} Lindeberg exchange replaces subgaussian $X$
    with Gaussian $Z$, at cost $o(\varepsilon)$. Uses negative correlation
    inequalities.
  \item \textbf{Rescaling.} Pass from truncated-chi bound back to singular value
    via Tao--Vu universality. The Gaussian case gives the optimal constant.
\end{enumerate}
}}
\end{center}

The loss of $1 + o(1)$ comes from the truncation step and the Lindeberg step, each
contributing a $(\log n)^{-c}$ error. Getting the constant exactly to~$1$ would
require either eliminating the truncation (which seems hard, since the full chi
statistic is too unwieldy for Lindeberg) or finding a completely different approach.

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

The Sah--Sahasrabudhe--Sawhney result closes a chapter that ran from Edelman's 1988
density computation through Rudelson--Vershynin, Tao--Vu, and Tikhomirov. The least
singular value of a random matrix with iid subgaussian entries satisfies
$\Prob(\sigma_n \le \varepsilon n^{-1/2}) \le (1 + o(1))\varepsilon$, matching the
Gaussian prediction up to a vanishing multiplicative error.

What remains? The $o(1)$ term. It decays as $(\log n)^{-1/16}$, which is glacially
slow. Getting the constant exactly to~$1$ would mean proving that the Rademacher (or
any other subgaussian) least singular value distribution matches the Gaussian one
with no error, not just vanishing error. That's a qualitatively different statement.
The current proof loses the $o(1)$ at the truncation step and again at Lindeberg
exchange. A proof that avoids both losses would probably need to bypass the ``reduce
to anti-concentration, then Gaussianize'' paradigm entirely.

The universality statement (Theorem~\ref{thm:sss-universality}) is, in a way, the
deeper result. It says the full distribution of $\sigma_n$ is asymptotically the same
for all subgaussian entry distributions. Our experiments confirm this: the Gaussian
and Rademacher CDFs are visually identical by $n = 200$.

From a practical standpoint, the bound confirms what numerical analysts have long
observed. Random matrices are well-conditioned. Small perturbations of structured
matrices are well-conditioned. The condition number of $M + \sigma G$ scales as
$O(n/\sigma)$, and this is tight. The smoothed analysis framework of Spielman
and Teng now rests on an essentially optimal theoretical foundation.
