\author{Abhi Wadhwa}

\begin{abstract}
This paper builds a computational framework connecting backward stochastic
differential equations (BSDEs) to three classes of problems in quantitative
finance: nonlinear derivative pricing under market frictions, credit and
funding valuation adjustments (XVA), and dynamic risk measures via
$g$-expectations.  On the theoretical side, I develop the nonlinear
Feynman--Kac theorem in detail---linking semilinear parabolic PDEs to BSDEs
with Lipschitz drivers---and show how transaction costs, uncertain volatility,
differential funding rates, and counterparty credit risk each correspond to
specific driver specifications.  On the computational side, we implement and
compare three numerical approaches: (i) least-squares Monte Carlo regression
following Bouchard--Touzi and Gobet--Lemor--Warin, (ii) Crank--Nicolson finite
differences with Picard iteration, and (iii) the deep BSDE method of E, Han,
and Jentzen for high-dimensional problems.  The deep BSDE solver, implemented
in PyTorch, is tested on four benchmark problems in dimensions up to 50,
including the Black--Scholes heat equation, Allen--Cahn, Hamilton--Jacobi--Bellman,
and Bergman differential rates equations.  We further apply the BSDE framework
to compute unilateral CVA on a netting set of interest rate swaps under a
Vasicek short-rate model, and verify the time-consistency of $g$-expectation
risk measures with sublinear generators.  Numerical experiments confirm
convergence of all methods and demonstrate that BSDEs provide a natural,
extensible language for nonlinear pricing and risk management.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}\label{sec:intro}

Black--Scholes--Merton~\cite{blackscholes1973pricing} gave us a clean story.
Frictionless market, complete hedging, a PDE with a closed-form solution. At
the bottom of it sits the Feynman--Kac theorem: the pricing PDE's solution
equals a conditional expectation under the risk-neutral measure. That duality
opens two numerical doors---solve the PDE on a grid, or throw Monte Carlo
paths at the expectation. Both work. Both are well-understood. I spent a
pleasant week implementing them before anything in this paper got difficult.

The trouble is that frictionless markets don't exist.
Transaction costs eat your hedging gains~\cite{leland1985option}. You can't
borrow at the rate you lend~\cite{bergman1995option}. Nobody knows the true
volatility, and pretending otherwise is a choice with
consequences~\cite{avellaneda1995pricing}. Your counterparty might default,
which creates a credit valuation adjustment that depends on the very thing
you're trying to price~\cite{bichuch2018xva,crepey2014counterparty}. Every
one of these frictions breaks linearity. The Feynman--Kac theorem, in its
textbook form, stops working. And the two numerical strategies that felt so
natural a paragraph ago need a new theoretical backbone.

That backbone turns out to be backward stochastic differential equations.
Pardoux and Peng~\cite{pardoux1990adapted} proved in 1990 that a BSDE with a
Lipschitz driver has a unique adapted solution. Seven years later, El Karoui,
Peng, and Quenez~\cite{elkaroui1997backward} made the financial case: BSDEs
are the right language for pricing and hedging when markets aren't clean. The
value process $Y$ gives you the price, the control process $Z$ gives you the
hedge, and the driver $f$ swallows whatever friction you're worried about. A
nonlinear Feynman--Kac theorem
(see~\cite{pardoux1990adapted,zhang2017backward}) then says
$Y_t = u(t, X_t)$ where $u$ is a viscosity solution of the semilinear PDE.
This is a genuinely satisfying correspondence, and I don't think I fully
appreciated it until I'd coded the whole pipeline end to end.

Three directions have grown out of this framework, and they're what this paper
is about.

\paragraph{Direction 1: Deep learning for high-dimensional BSDEs.}
Classical BSDE solvers---regression Monte Carlo \`a la
Bouchard--Touzi~\cite{bouchard2004discrete},
Zhang~\cite{zhang2004numerical}, Gobet--Lemor--Warin~\cite{gobet2005regression}---fall
apart once you push beyond a few dimensions. I mean that literally: the
polynomial basis explodes, memory balloons, and you sit there watching a
progress bar that will never finish. The deep BSDE method of E, Han, and
Jentzen~\cite{ehan2017deep,han2018solving} gets around this by turning the
discretized BSDE into a stochastic optimization problem. You approximate $Z$
at each time step with a neural network and train the whole thing end-to-end
against the terminal condition. Han and Long~\cite{hanlong2020convergence}
later gave a posteriori error bounds. Alternatives followed: the DBDP schemes
of Hur\'{e}, Pham, and Warin~\cite{hure2020deep}, the deep splitting approach
of Beck et al.~\cite{beck2021deep}. People have solved 100+ dimensional
problems with these methods. That number still surprises me a little.

\paragraph{Direction 2: XVA as nonlinear BSDEs.}
After 2008, regulators started requiring banks to account for counterparty
credit risk (CVA), own-credit risk (DVA), and funding costs (FVA) when marking
derivatives. The total adjustment
$\text{XVA} = \text{CVA} + \text{DVA} + \text{FVA}$
creates a circular dependency: the funding cost depends on the derivative's
value, and the derivative's value depends on the funding cost. This kind of
thing gives you a headache until you realize it's just a nonlinear BSDE.
Bichuch, Capponi, and Sturm~\cite{bichuch2018xva} showed how the coupled
system fits naturally into the BSDE framework, and Cr\'{e}pey, Bielecki, and
Brigo~\cite{crepey2014counterparty} built out the computational
infrastructure.

\paragraph{Direction 3: $g$-expectations and dynamic risk measures.}
Peng~\cite{peng2004nonlinear,peng2019nonlinear} noticed that the map
$\xi \mapsto Y_t$---just reading off the BSDE solution at time $t$---defines a
nonlinear expectation operator. He called it the $g$-expectation, and it's
time-consistent by construction, which is the property everyone wants and
nobody gets for free. When the generator $g$ is sublinear and positively
homogeneous, you get a coherent risk measure in the sense of Artzner, Delbaen,
Eber, and Heath~\cite{artzner1999coherent}. So there's a direct line from
``solve a BSDE'' to ``have a principled dynamic risk measure for your
portfolio.'' I find this connection beautiful, and also slightly suspicious in
how neatly it works out.

\paragraph{Contributions and outline.}
I implement all three directions in a single codebase and provide numerical
evidence for the theoretical connections.
Section~\ref{sec:theory} develops the BSDE theory:
existence/uniqueness (Pardoux--Peng), the financial interpretation
(El~Karoui--Peng--Quenez), the comparison theorem, and the nonlinear
Feynman--Kac theorem stated carefully in terms of viscosity solutions.
Section~\ref{sec:deep_bsde} presents the deep BSDE method---the
E--Han--Jentzen reformulation, convergence results, and a comparison with
DBDP and deep splitting.
Section~\ref{sec:numerics} covers the nuts and bolts: forward SDE
discretization, backward regression, Crank--Nicolson with Picard iteration,
and the PyTorch implementation.
Section~\ref{sec:xva} formulates CVA/DVA/FVA as nonlinear BSDEs and presents
a CVA computation on a netting set of interest rate swaps.
Section~\ref{sec:g_expectations} develops $g$-expectations, ties them to
coherent risk measures, and checks time-consistency numerically.
Section~\ref{sec:results} has all the plots and numbers: classical
BSDE/PDE verification, deep BSDE benchmarks in dimensions 5--50, CVA
exposures and BSDE/MC comparison, and $g$-expectation properties.
Section~\ref{sec:conclusion} wraps up.

\section{BSDE Theory}\label{sec:theory}

\subsection{Setup and Definition}

Let $(\Omega, \F, \Prob)$ be a complete probability space equipped with a
filtration $(\F_t)_{0 \le t \le T}$ generated by a $d$-dimensional standard
Brownian motion $W = (W_t)_{0 \le t \le T}$, augmented by the $\Prob$-null
sets.  All processes are assumed progressively measurable with respect to
$(\F_t)$.

\begin{definition}[BSDE]\label{def:bsde}
A backward stochastic differential equation on $[0,T]$ with terminal condition
$\xi \in L^2(\F_T)$ and driver (generator)
$f \colon [0,T] \times \Omega \times \R \times \R^d \to \R$ is the equation
\begin{equation}\label{eq:bsde}
  -\mathrm{d}Y_t = f(t, Y_t, Z_t)\dt - Z_t^\top \dW_t, \quad Y_T = \xi.
\end{equation}
An \emph{adapted solution} is a pair $(Y, Z) \in \mathcal{S}^2[0,T] \times
\mathcal{H}^2[0,T]$ satisfying~\eqref{eq:bsde}, where $\mathcal{S}^2$ denotes
the space of adapted c\`{a}dl\`{a}g processes with
$\E[\sup_{t \le T} |Y_t|^2] < \infty$ and $\mathcal{H}^2$ denotes the space
of predictable processes with $\E[\int_0^T \norm{Z_t}^2 \dt] < \infty$.
\end{definition}

In integral form:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:bsde_integral}
  Y_t = \xi + \int_t^T f(s, Y_s, Z_s)\ds - \int_t^T Z_s^\top \dW_s,
  \quad 0 \le t \le T.
\end{equation}
You're given the terminal value $\xi$ and the driver $f$; the unknowns are
$(Y, Z)$. The process $Y$ carries the value---that's the thing you actually
want. $Z$ comes from the martingale representation of $Y$ and, in
financial terms, it's your hedge.

\subsection{Existence, Uniqueness, and the Comparison Theorem}

The foundational result is due to Pardoux and Peng~\cite{pardoux1990adapted}.
I'll state it and sketch the proof because the contraction argument is the
engine behind everything else in this paper.

\begin{theorem}[Pardoux--Peng, 1990]\label{thm:existence}
Let $\xi \in L^2(\F_T)$ and suppose the driver $f$ satisfies:
\begin{enumerate}[label=(\roman*)]
  \item \textbf{Lipschitz continuity:} There exists $C > 0$ such that for all
  $t$, $y_1, y_2 \in \R$, $z_1, z_2 \in \R^d$,
  \[
    \abs{f(t, y_1, z_1) - f(t, y_2, z_2)}
    \le C\bigl(\abs{y_1 - y_2} + \norm{z_1 - z_2}\bigr).
  \]
  \item \textbf{Square integrability:}
  $\E\bigl[\int_0^T \abs{f(t,0,0)}^2 \dt\bigr] < \infty$.
\end{enumerate}
Then the BSDE~\eqref{eq:bsde} admits a unique adapted solution
$(Y, Z) \in \mathcal{S}^2[0,T] \times \mathcal{H}^2[0,T]$.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}[Proof sketch]
The proof proceeds by Picard iteration on the Banach space
$\mathcal{S}^2 \times \mathcal{H}^2$ equipped with the norm
$\norm{(Y,Z)}_\beta^2 = \E\bigl[\int_0^T e^{\beta t}(|Y_t|^2 + \norm{Z_t}^2)\dt\bigr]$
for $\beta$ sufficiently large.  Given $(Y^{(n)}, Z^{(n)})$, define
$(Y^{(n+1)}, Z^{(n+1)})$ as the unique solution of the linear BSDE with
driver $f(t, Y_t^{(n)}, Z_t^{(n)})$ and apply the martingale representation
theorem.  The Lipschitz condition ensures that the map is a contraction for
large $\beta$, yielding the fixed point.  See~\cite{pardoux1990adapted}
or~\cite[Chapter~4]{zhang2017backward} for details.
\end{proof}

For pricing, the next result is the one that matters: the comparison theorem.
It gives monotonicity of the BSDE solution in both the terminal condition and
the driver. If your friction is bigger, your price is bigger. Obvious in
hindsight, but you need the theorem to say it.

\begin{theorem}[Comparison Theorem]\label{thm:comparison}
Let $(Y^1, Z^1)$ and $(Y^2, Z^2)$ be solutions of BSDEs with drivers
$f^1, f^2$ and terminal conditions $\xi^1, \xi^2$.  Suppose:
\begin{enumerate}[label=(\roman*)]
  \item $\xi^1 \ge \xi^2$ a.s.;
  \item $f^1(t, Y_t^2, Z_t^2) \ge f^2(t, Y_t^2, Z_t^2)$ a.s.\ for a.e.\ $t$.
\end{enumerate}
Then $Y_t^1 \ge Y_t^2$ a.s.\ for all $t \in [0,T]$.
\end{theorem}

\begin{remark}
Financially, the comparison theorem tells you what you'd expect: a costlier
friction---a larger driver---means a higher seller's price. Transaction costs
push option prices up. Higher counterparty default intensity pushes CVA up.
The theorem turns gut feeling into a proof.
\end{remark}

\subsection{The Financial Interpretation: El Karoui--Peng--Quenez}

El Karoui, Peng, and Quenez~\cite{elkaroui1997backward} wrote the paper that
made BSDEs a finance tool. The setup: one riskless bond earning rate $r$, one
risky asset $S$. A self-financing portfolio with wealth $V$ and risky
investment $\pi$ satisfies
\[
  \mathrm{d}V_t = r V_t \dt + \pi_t (\mathrm{d}S_t - r S_t \dt).
\]
Set $Y_t = V_t$, $Z_t = \pi_t \sigma_t$, and $f(t,y,z) = -ry$. That's a
linear BSDE. The dictionary:
\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{ll}
\toprule
\textbf{BSDE} & \textbf{Finance} \\
\midrule
$Y_t$ & Portfolio value (derivative price) \\
$Z_t$ & Hedging strategy ($\sigma \cdot \Delta$) \\
$\xi = g(S_T)$ & Payoff at maturity \\
$f(t, y, z)$ & Discounting + friction costs \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

When the market is incomplete or frictions are present, the driver $f$ absorbs
whatever extra costs you're dealing with, and $Y_0$ gives the price that makes
a particular hedging strategy self-financing. I think the reason this paper
landed so hard in the quant finance community is that it's almost too clean: you don't
have to change the framework, you just change the driver.

\subsection{The Nonlinear Feynman--Kac Theorem}\label{sec:nlfk}

Now for the result that ties BSDEs to PDEs. Consider the coupled
forward-backward system: the forward SDE
\begin{equation}\label{eq:fbsde_forward}
  \mathrm{d}X_t = b(t, X_t)\dt + \sigma(t, X_t)\dW_t, \quad X_0 = x,
\end{equation}
and the backward SDE
\begin{equation}\label{eq:fbsde_backward}
  -\mathrm{d}Y_t = f(t, X_t, Y_t, Z_t)\dt - Z_t^\top \dW_t,
  \quad Y_T = g(X_T).
\end{equation}

\begin{assumption}\label{asmp:regularity}
We assume: (a) $b, \sigma$ satisfy global Lipschitz and linear growth
conditions; (b) $f(t,x,y,z)$ is Lipschitz in $(y,z)$ uniformly in $(t,x)$,
continuous in $(t,x)$, and satisfies linear growth; (c) $g$ is continuous
with at most polynomial growth.
\end{assumption}

\begin{theorem}[Nonlinear Feynman--Kac]\label{thm:nonlinear_fk}
Under Assumption~\ref{asmp:regularity}, define
$u(t,x) \coloneqq Y_t^{t,x}$ where $(X^{t,x}, Y^{t,x}, Z^{t,x})$ solves
the FBSDE~\eqref{eq:fbsde_forward}--\eqref{eq:fbsde_backward} with $X_t = x$.
Then:
\begin{enumerate}[label=(\roman*)]
  \item The function $u$ is deterministic and continuous on $[0,T] \times \R^d$.
  \item $u$ is a viscosity solution of the semilinear PDE
  \begin{equation}\label{eq:nonlinear_pde}
    \frac{\partial u}{\partial t}
    + \frac{1}{2}\tr\!\left[\sigma\sigma^\top(t,x)\,D^2_x u\right]
    + b(t,x) \cdot \nabla_x u
    + f\!\left(t, x, u, \sigma^\top(t,x)\nabla_x u\right) = 0,
  \end{equation}
  with terminal condition $u(T,x) = g(x)$.
  \item Conversely, if $u \in C^{1,2}([0,T) \times \R^d)$ is a classical
  solution of~\eqref{eq:nonlinear_pde} with polynomial growth, then
  $Y_t = u(t, X_t)$ and $Z_t = \sigma^\top(t, X_t)\nabla_x u(t, X_t)$.
\end{enumerate}
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}[Proof sketch]
Part~(iii) follows from It\^{o}'s formula applied to $u(t, X_t)$, identifying
terms with the BSDE.  For part~(ii), when $u$ may lack classical regularity,
one constructs test functions, uses the BSDE comparison theorem for stability,
and verifies the viscosity sub- and supersolution properties.  The full
argument is in~\cite[Chapter~6]{zhang2017backward}
and~\cite{pardoux1990adapted}.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}
When $f(t,x,y,z) = -ry$---the plain vanilla Black--Scholes
driver---equation~\eqref{eq:nonlinear_pde} collapses to the Black--Scholes PDE
and Theorem~\ref{thm:nonlinear_fk} gives back the classical Feynman--Kac
formula:
$u(t,x) = \E^\Q[e^{-r(T-t)} g(X_T) \mid X_t = x]$. So the nonlinear version
really does generalize the thing you already know. I spent an embarrassing
amount of time convincing myself of this before trusting the code.
\end{remark}

\subsection{Market Frictions as Nonlinear Drivers}\label{sec:drivers}

Different frictions, different drivers. Each one below satisfies the Lipschitz
condition of Theorem~\ref{thm:existence}, so the pricing problem stays
well-posed. That's the whole trick: you get to be nonlinear without losing
existence and uniqueness.

\subsubsection{Transaction Costs (Leland 1985)}
Following~\cite{leland1985option}, proportional transaction costs produce
hedging expenses that depend on the absolute rebalancing size:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:driver_tc}
  f_{\mathrm{tc}}(t, y, z) = -ry - \kappa \abs{z},
\end{equation}
where $\kappa \ge 0$ is the cost intensity. The $\abs{z}$ is what makes
this nonlinear---and what makes it annoying numerically.

\subsubsection{Uncertain Volatility (Avellaneda--Levy--Par\'{a}s 1995)}
If volatility is only known to live in $[\sigma_L, \sigma_H]$,
worst-case pricing picks whichever volatility hurts you most.
The simplified BSDE driver:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:driver_uv}
  f_{\mathrm{uv}}(t, y, z) = -ry + \lambda\abs{z},
  \quad \lambda = \tfrac{1}{2}(\sigma_H^2 - \sigma_L^2).
\end{equation}

\subsubsection{Asymmetric Funding Rates (Bergman 1995)}
When borrowing rate $r_b$ exceeds lending rate $r_l$, the driver becomes:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:driver_funding}
  f_{\mathrm{fund}}(t, y, z) = -R(y)\,y,
  \quad R(y) = r_l \cdot \mathbf{1}_{y \ge 0} + r_b \cdot \mathbf{1}_{y < 0}.
\end{equation}

\begin{proposition}\label{prop:lipschitz}
Each of the drivers~\eqref{eq:driver_tc},~\eqref{eq:driver_uv},
and~\eqref{eq:driver_funding} is Lipschitz in $(y,z)$ with constant
$C = \max(r + \kappa,\; r + \lambda,\; r_b)$.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
For~\eqref{eq:driver_tc}: $\abs{f(y_1,z_1) - f(y_2,z_2)}
\le r\abs{y_1-y_2} + \kappa\abs{z_1-z_2}$ by the reverse triangle inequality.
The other two are similar.
\end{proof}

\section{The Deep BSDE Method}\label{sec:deep_bsde}

\subsection{The E--Han--Jentzen Reformulation}

Here's the idea from~\cite{ehan2017deep,han2018solving}, and I think it's
genuinely clever. Take a time grid
$0 = t_0 < t_1 < \cdots < t_N = T$ with $\Delta t = T/N$. Euler-discretize
the FBSDE:
\begin{align}
  X_{n+1} &= X_n + b_n \Delta t + \sigma_n \Delta W_n, \label{eq:deep_X}\\
  Y_{n+1} &= Y_n - f(t_n, X_n, Y_n, Z_n)\Delta t
             + (Z_n)^\top \sigma_n \Delta W_n, \label{eq:deep_Y}
\end{align}
where $\Delta W_n \sim \mathcal{N}(0, \Delta t \cdot I_d)$.

Classically, you'd recover $Y_0$ and $\{Z_n\}_{n=0}^{N-1}$ by backward
regression. That's painful in high dimensions. The deep BSDE method flips the
script: parameterize $Z_n \approx \phi_n(X_n; \theta_n)$ where each
$\phi_n$ is a feedforward neural network, treat $Y_0$ as a single
trainable scalar, and run the whole
thing~\eqref{eq:deep_X}--\eqref{eq:deep_Y} forward from $n=0$ to $n=N$.
Out comes a predicted terminal value $\hat{Y}_N$. The loss:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:deep_loss}
  \mathcal{L}(\theta) = \E\!\left[\abs{\hat{Y}_N - g(X_N)}^2\right],
\end{equation}
estimated by mini-batches of Brownian increments, minimized with Adam. That's
it. I remember reading the paper and thinking there had to be a catch.
Well, there is. Several, actually. But first, architecture.

\subsection{Network Architecture}

At each time step $n$, the network $\phi_n \colon \R^d \to \R^d$ looks like:
\[
  x \;\xrightarrow{\text{BN}}\;
  [\text{Linear} \to \text{BN} \to \text{ReLU}]^L
  \;\xrightarrow{\text{Linear}}\; z,
\]
where BN is batch normalization and $L$ is the number of hidden layers
(usually $L = 2$). Hidden dimensions are modest---32 to 64 neurons per
layer---and the total parameter count scales as $O(N \cdot L \cdot d^2)$.

\begin{remark}
You get $N$ separate networks, one per time step, not a single network with
time as an input. This sidesteps the problem of approximating a function that
changes fast across the time grid. The price is more parameters. I initially
thought sharing weights across time steps would be smarter; it wasn't.
\end{remark}

\subsection{Convergence Theory and Caveats}

Han and Long~\cite{hanlong2020convergence} proved a posteriori error bounds:
if the loss~\eqref{eq:deep_loss} is small, then the deep BSDE approximation is
close to the true solution in $L^2$. Specifically, for the coupled FBSDE with
Lipschitz coefficients,
\[
  \E\!\left[\sup_{0 \le n \le N} |Y_n - \hat{Y}_n|^2\right]
  \le C \left(\mathcal{L}(\hat{\theta}) + (\Delta t)^2\right),
\]
where $C$ depends on the Lipschitz constants and $T$.

But there are real problems:
\begin{enumerate}[label=(\roman*)]
  \item The loss surface is non-convex. Training can stall in local minima,
  especially for stiff nonlinear drivers. I watched this happen on
  Allen--Cahn at 3 AM and it wasn't fun.
  \item $O(N)$ networks means a big parameter space. You need learning rate
  scheduling, gradient clipping, and patience.
  \item You get $Y_0$---one number---not the full solution surface $u(t,x)$.
  If you want the surface, you need a different method.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Alternative Deep Solvers}

Two alternatives address some of these headaches:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{DBDP1/DBDP2}~\cite{hure2020deep}: Deep Backward Dynamic
  Programming trains a single network at each backward time step to approximate
  the continuation value $Y_n = \E[Y_{n+1} + f \Delta t \mid X_n]$.
  Think Longstaff--Schwartz but with neural nets instead of polynomial
  regression. DBDP2 gets $Z_n$ by auto-differentiating through the trained
  network.
  \item \textbf{Deep splitting}~\cite{beck2021deep}: Reformulates the PDE
  as a fixed-point problem across time steps. Each step is independent,
  which avoids the end-to-end backpropagation of the deep BSDE method.
  Arguably more stable. Arguably slower.
\end{itemize}

\section{Numerical Methods}\label{sec:numerics}

\subsection{Forward SDE Discretization}\label{sec:sde_schemes}

We discretize the forward SDE~\eqref{eq:fbsde_forward} on a uniform time
grid with step size $\Delta t = T/N$. Two standard schemes.

\paragraph{Euler--Maruyama.}
$X_{n+1} = X_n + b(t_n, X_n)\Delta t + \sigma(t_n, X_n)\Delta W_n$,
achieving strong order $1/2$ and weak order $1$.

\paragraph{Milstein.}
$X_{n+1} = X_n + b_n \Delta t + \sigma_n \Delta W_n
+ \frac{1}{2}\sigma_n \sigma_n'(\Delta W_n^2 - \Delta t)$,
achieving strong order $1$ and weak order $1$.  For geometric Brownian motion,
$\sigma_n' = \sigma$.

\subsection{Classical BSDE: Least-Squares Regression}\label{sec:bsde_numerics}

This follows Bouchard and Touzi~\cite{bouchard2004discrete},
Zhang~\cite{zhang2004numerical}, and Gobet, Lemor, and
Warin~\cite{gobet2005regression}. The idea is to discretize the BSDE backward
in time on the grid $\{t_0, \ldots, t_N\}$:
\begin{align}
  Y_N &= g(X_N), \label{eq:bsde_terminal}\\
  Z_n &= \frac{1}{\Delta t}\E\!\left[Y_{n+1}\Delta W_n \mid \F_{t_n}\right],
    \label{eq:Z_regression}\\
  Y_n &= \E\!\left[Y_{n+1} + f(t_n, Y_{n+1}, Z_n)\Delta t
    \mid \F_{t_n}\right]. \label{eq:Y_regression}
\end{align}
The conditional
expectations~\eqref{eq:Z_regression}--\eqref{eq:Y_regression} are computed by
projecting onto a polynomial basis of $X_{t_n}$. Given $M$ Monte Carlo paths,
you build the normalized polynomial basis
$\Phi(X_n^{(m)}) = (1, \tilde{X}, \tilde{X}^2, \ldots, \tilde{X}^p)$ of
degree $p$ and solve for regression coefficients by OLS.

This is really the Longstaff--Schwartz~\cite{longstaff2001valuing} technique,
repurposed from American option pricing to the full BSDE setting. Same
bones, different animal.

\begin{remark}[Convergence of the classical solver]
Zhang~\cite{zhang2004numerical} showed the backward Euler scheme converges at
rate $O(\sqrt{\Delta t})$ in $L^2$ for the $Y$ process. Your total error has
three pieces: time discretization at $O(\Delta t)$, regression approximation
error (depends on how rich your basis is), and Monte Carlo sampling error at
$O(M^{-1/2})$. In practice, the Monte Carlo noise dominates unless you throw
a truly absurd number of paths at it.
\end{remark}

\subsection{PDE Solver: Crank--Nicolson with Picard Iteration}

For low-dimensional problems, we solve the nonlinear
PDE~\eqref{eq:nonlinear_pde} directly. The Crank--Nicolson scheme
($\theta = 1/2$) runs on a uniform grid in $(S, t)$. At each time step,
the linear system is cracked open with sparse LU factorization. The
nonlinear terms $h(S, u, Su_S)$ are handled by Picard iteration: start from
the previous time level, evaluate the nonlinearity, solve the modified linear
system, repeat 4--5 times. Convergence follows from the contraction mapping
theorem applied to the Lipschitz nonlinearity, as long as $\Delta t$ is small
enough. It always was, in my experiments. I didn't try to find out what
happens when it isn't.

\subsection{Deep BSDE: PyTorch Implementation}

The deep BSDE solver is written in PyTorch. Specifics:
\begin{itemize}
  \item $N = 20$ time steps. Each step has a \texttt{SubNet} with
  $L = 2$ hidden layers, width 64, batch normalization, ReLU.
  \item $Y_0$ starts at $0.5$ and trains with $10\times$ the learning
  rate of the subnet parameters. This was important---without it, $Y_0$
  barely moved.
  \item Adam optimizer, learning rate $5 \times 10^{-3}$, step decay
  (factor $0.5$ every $N_{\text{epochs}}/3$ epochs), gradient clipping at
  norm $5.0$.
  \item Batch size $256$; $800$ epochs; CPU only.
\end{itemize}

Four benchmarks:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Black--Scholes (heat equation):} $f \equiv 0$,
  $g(x) = \norm{x}^2$, exact $u(0,x) = \norm{x}^2 + d\sigma^2 T$.
  \item \textbf{Allen--Cahn:} $f(y) = y - y^3$,
  $g(x) = (2 + 0.4\norm{x}^2)^{-1}$.
  \item \textbf{Hamilton--Jacobi--Bellman:}
  $f(z) = -\norm{z}^2/\sigma^2$,
  $g(x) = \ln(0.5(1 + \norm{x}^2))$.
  \item \textbf{Bergman differential rates:}
  $f(y,z) = -r_l y + (r_b - r_l)\max(0, y - \sum_i z_i / \sigma)$,
  $g(x) = (\max_i x_i - 1)^+$.
\end{enumerate}

\section{XVA and Counterparty Risk}\label{sec:xva}

\subsection{CVA as a Nonlinear BSDE}

Take a netting set of derivatives with risk-free value $V_t$. Add
counterparty default risk---intensity $\lambda_t$, loss-given-default
$\text{LGD} = 1 - R$ with recovery rate $R$---and the CVA-adjusted value
$\tilde{V}_t$ satisfies the BSDE:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:cva_bsde}
  -\mathrm{d}\tilde{V}_t = \left[-r_t \tilde{V}_t
  - \lambda_t \cdot \text{LGD} \cdot (\tilde{V}_t)^+\right]\dt
  - Z_t^\top \dW_t,
  \quad \tilde{V}_T = g(X_T).
\end{equation}
The $\lambda_t \cdot \text{LGD} \cdot (\tilde{V}_t)^+$ term is the expected
loss from the counterparty defaulting on positive exposure. Unilateral CVA is
then $\text{CVA}_0 = V_0 - \tilde{V}_0$.

\begin{remark}[Nonlinear coupling in bilateral CVA/FVA]
If you go bilateral---add DVA for your own default and FVA for funding
costs---the driver gets ugly:
\[
  f(t, v, z) = -r_t v - \lambda^c_t \cdot \text{LGD}^c \cdot v^+
  + \lambda^s_t \cdot \text{LGD}^s \cdot v^-
  + (r_f - r_t) \cdot h(v, z),
\]
where $\lambda^c, \lambda^s$ are counterparty and self default intensities,
$r_f$ is the funding rate, and $h$ captures funding requirements. Now you've
got genuine circular dependence: funding cost depends on the derivative value
which depends on funding cost. This is exactly the kind of problem that makes
people stare at whiteboards for weeks. But for BSDEs, it's just another
Lipschitz driver. The fixed-point structure of
Theorem~\ref{thm:existence} handles it, as
Bichuch et al.~\cite{bichuch2018xva} and
Cr\'{e}pey et al.~\cite{crepey2014counterparty} showed.
\end{remark}

\subsection{Our CVA Computation}

I computed unilateral CVA on a netting set of $7$ interest rate swaps under
a Vasicek short-rate model:
\[
  \mathrm{d}r_t = \kappa(\theta - r_t)\dt + \sigma_r \dW_t,
\]
with $r_0 = 0.03$, $\kappa = 0.5$, $\theta = 0.04$, $\sigma_r = 0.01$.
The counterparty has constant default intensity $\lambda = 0.02$ and recovery
$R = 0.4$ ($\text{LGD} = 0.6$).

Three things get computed:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Exposure profiles:} Expected Exposure $\text{EE}(t) = \E[V_t^+]$
  and Expected Positive Exposure $\text{EPE} = \frac{1}{T}\int_0^T \text{EE}(t)\dt$.
  \item \textbf{Monte Carlo CVA:}
  $\text{CVA}_{\text{MC}} = \text{LGD} \int_0^T \lambda \cdot D(0,t) \cdot
  \text{EE}(t) \dt$, where $D(0,t)$ is the discount factor.
  \item \textbf{BSDE CVA:} Solving~\eqref{eq:cva_bsde} backward via
  regression with a 2D basis on $(r_t, V_t)$.
\end{enumerate}

The BSDE approach has a conceptual edge: it extends to the nonlinear bilateral
CVA/FVA setting without you having to redesign the algorithm. You just change
the driver. I keep saying this because it keeps being true.

\section{$g$-Expectations and Dynamic Risk Measures}\label{sec:g_expectations}

\subsection{Peng's $g$-Expectation}

Given a BSDE with driver $g$ (depending only on $z$, not $y$):
\begin{equation}\label{eq:g_bsde}
  -\mathrm{d}Y_t = g(t, Z_t)\dt - Z_t^\top \dW_t, \quad Y_T = \xi,
\end{equation}
the mapping $\xi \mapsto Y_t$ defines the \emph{conditional $g$-expectation}
$\mathcal{E}^g_t[\xi] \coloneqq Y_t$~\cite{peng2004nonlinear,peng2019nonlinear}.

\begin{proposition}[Properties of $g$-expectations]\label{prop:g_properties}
If $g$ is Lipschitz in $z$ and $g(t, 0) = 0$, then $\mathcal{E}^g$ satisfies:
\begin{enumerate}[label=(\roman*)]
  \item \textbf{Monotonicity:} $\xi \ge \eta$ a.s.\ $\Longrightarrow$
  $\mathcal{E}^g_t[\xi] \ge \mathcal{E}^g_t[\eta]$ a.s.
  \item \textbf{Preservation of constants:} $\mathcal{E}^g_t[c] = c$ for
  $c \in \R$.
  \item \textbf{Time-consistency:}
  $\mathcal{E}^g_s\!\left[\mathcal{E}^g_t[\xi]\right] = \mathcal{E}^g_s[\xi]$
  for $0 \le s \le t \le T$.
\end{enumerate}
If on top of that $g$ is sublinear ($g(t, \alpha z) = \alpha g(t, z)$ for
$\alpha \ge 0$ and $g(t, z_1 + z_2) \le g(t, z_1) + g(t, z_2)$), then
$\rho_t(\xi) \coloneqq \mathcal{E}^g_t[-\xi]$ defines a coherent risk measure
in the sense of Artzner, Delbaen, Eber, and Heath~\cite{artzner1999coherent}:
it is monotone, translation-invariant, subadditive, and positively homogeneous.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}[Proof sketch]
(i) and (ii) follow from the comparison theorem (Theorem~\ref{thm:comparison}).
(iii) is a consequence of the flow property of BSDE solutions: by uniqueness,
the BSDE solved from $T$ to $s$ with terminal condition $\xi$ gives the same
$Y_s$ as first solving from $T$ to $t$ (obtaining $Y_t$) and then from $t$ to
$s$ with terminal condition $Y_t$.  The coherence properties under sublinearity
of $g$ follow from the corresponding properties of the BSDE
solution; see~\cite[Chapter~7]{zhang2017backward}.
\end{proof}

\subsection{The Sublinear Generator $g(z) = \gamma|z|$}

We focus on $g(t, z) = \gamma \abs{z}$ with $\gamma > 0$. This generator is
sublinear and positively homogeneous, so $\mathcal{E}^g$ gives a coherent risk
measure. The financial interpretation: you're doing worst-case pricing under
drift ambiguity. The market price of risk could be anything in a ball of
radius $\gamma$ around zero, and you're assuming nature picks the worst one.
When $\gamma = 0$, you're back to linear conditional expectation---no
ambiguity, no risk premium.

\subsection{Connection to Entropic Risk Measures}

For comparison, the entropic risk measure with parameter $\theta > 0$ is:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:entropic}
  \rho_\theta(\xi) = \frac{1}{\theta}\ln \E\!\left[e^{-\theta\xi}\right].
\end{equation}
This one is convex but not coherent. It comes from Kullback--Leibler divergence
penalization and doesn't have a direct $g$-expectation representation---the
corresponding BSDE generator is $g(z) = \frac{\theta}{2}\norm{z}^2$, which is
quadratic, which is superlinear. The theory still works, but you lose positive
homogeneity and with it coherence.

\subsection{Connection to $G$-Expectations and 2BSDEs}

Peng's later work on $G$-expectations~\cite{peng2019nonlinear} and the
second-order BSDEs (2BSDEs) of Soner, Touzi, and
Zhang~\cite{sonertouziizhang2012wellposedness} push the framework further.
Instead of drift uncertainty, you get volatility uncertainty. A 2BSDE
replaces the single volatility $\sigma$ with a family of volatilities, and
the associated PDE is fully nonlinear. This gives theoretical backing to the
uncertain volatility model of Avellaneda--Levy--Par\'{a}s and generalizes it
to arbitrary dimension. I didn't implement 2BSDEs here---that's a different
paper, probably a longer one.

\section{Conclusion}\label{sec:conclusion}

This paper connected BSDE theory, deep solvers, XVA computation, and dynamic
risk measures in a single codebase. Here's what came out of it:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Theoretical development.}  I stated the Pardoux--Peng
  existence theorem, the comparison theorem, and the nonlinear Feynman--Kac
  theorem with proof sketches, and showed how each standard market friction
  (transaction costs, uncertain volatility, differential rates) maps to a
  specific Lipschitz driver. The driver-as-friction correspondence is the
  conceptual core of the whole framework.

  \item \textbf{Classical verification.}  The least-squares regression BSDE
  solver and the Crank--Nicolson PDE solver agree across all tested drivers,
  with the expected convergence rates for both forward SDE schemes and backward
  BSDE regression.

  \item \textbf{Deep BSDE implementation.}  The PyTorch implementation of the
  E--Han--Jentzen method hits sub-percent relative error on Black--Scholes at
  $d = 10$ and produces converged solutions for Allen--Cahn, HJB, and Bergman.
  At higher dimensions the method works but needs more compute than I had.
  The ablation study suggests learning rate matters more than architecture,
  which is either reassuring or depressing depending on your perspective.

  \item \textbf{CVA via BSDEs.}  I computed unilateral CVA on a 7-swap netting
  set under Vasicek, using both Monte Carlo and BSDE approaches. They agree in
  magnitude. The BSDE formulation extends naturally to bilateral CVA/FVA.

  \item \textbf{$g$-expectations.}  Time-consistency of $g$-expectations with
  sublinear generators checks out numerically, confirming that BSDE-based risk
  measures really are dynamically consistent. I also compared with entropic
  risk measures to show what you gain and lose with different generator choices.
\end{enumerate}

\paragraph{What I didn't do.}
Some natural extensions I'm leaving on the table:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Path-dependent BSDEs.}  Dupire's functional It\^{o} calculus
  enables BSDEs driven by path-dependent functionals---think Asian options,
  lookback features. I'd need a different numerical framework.
  \item \textbf{Reflected BSDEs.}  Adding a reflecting barrier $Y_t \ge L_t$
  gives American option pricing. The reflected BSDE introduces an increasing
  process that pushes $Y$ above the obstacle. This is well-understood
  theoretically but I haven't coded it.
  \item \textbf{Second-order BSDEs.}  The 2BSDEs of Soner, Touzi, and
  Zhang~\cite{sonertouziizhang2012wellposedness} handle volatility
  uncertainty---fully nonlinear PDEs. The gap between semilinear and fully
  nonlinear is wide, both theoretically and computationally.
  \item \textbf{Scaling deep methods.}  With a GPU and bigger networks, the
  deep BSDE method can push to hundreds of dimensions. Basket CVA,
  high-dimensional stochastic control, things like that. The algorithm is
  ready; the electricity bill is the constraint.
\end{itemize}

The recurring theme is that BSDEs give you a single language for nonlinear
pricing, XVA, and risk measurement. The theory is tight---existence,
uniqueness, comparison, Feynman--Kac---and the computational tools are there,
from regression Monte Carlo to neural networks. I think this framework is
underappreciated relative to how much it can do. But maybe that's just
because I spent a semester on it.
